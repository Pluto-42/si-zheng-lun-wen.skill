# Red Course Paper

## Core Principle

Write red-course papers as argument-driven coursework, not slogan compilations. Start from a real question, use course concepts accurately, connect them to historical or contemporary materials, then add style. Never let ornate wording replace analysis.

Use this order:

1. Identify the course type, topic, word count, required sources, and whether the task is drafting, revising, or de-AI polishing.
2. Build a central claim that is politically steady, conceptually precise, and narrow enough to argue.
3. Choose an argument structure from `references/argument-patterns.md`.
4. Use `references/course-map.md` to match the course's expected angle.
5. Use `references/style-and-diction.md` only after the argument is clear.
6. Run the paper through `references/anti-ai-checklist.md` before delivery.

## Evidence And Source Discipline

For recent laws, policies, plans, meetings, leaders, or official documents, verify current details from official sources before writing factual claims. Do not invent policy dates, document titles, quotations, personal experiences, survey data, or classroom requirements.

For classic theory claims, prefer course textbooks, Marx/Engels/Lenin/Mao/Deng/Jiang/Hu/Xi texts where relevant, and clearly named official documents. If the user gives no source material, ask for course requirements or write a source-light draft with explicit placeholders.

## Writing Posture

Keep the tone:

- Firm in political direction, but not stiff.
- Academic enough for university coursework, but not full of empty macro-language.
- Capable of warmth and personal observation when the course permits it.
- Restrained with figurative diction. A few strong phrases are better than a dense pile of "high-level replacements."

Avoid:

- "重要意义/深刻启示/时代价值" repeated without concrete argument.
- Ending every essay with sudden "新时代青年使命" uplift unless the paper has prepared that move.
- Copying idioms or ornate replacements mechanically.
- Turning every concept into a metaphor.

## Output Defaults

When drafting: provide a title, outline, full essay, and brief note on argument logic.

When revising: preserve the user's structure unless it is clearly weak; revise locally before rewriting wholesale.

When polishing or de-AI: keep the original claim and evidence, reduce empty elevation, vary sentence rhythm, and add concrete transitions.
