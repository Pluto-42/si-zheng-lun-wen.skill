# Anti-AI Checklist For Red-Course Papers

Run this after drafting or revising.

## Argument

- Is there a concrete central question, not just a topic?
- Does each section advance the claim rather than repeat the same position?
- Are course concepts explained in the writer's own words?
- Is there at least one specific historical, social, policy, campus, or personal example?
- Does the conclusion follow from the essay rather than suddenly raising slogans?

## Red-Course Cliche Audit

Revise if the paper relies on:

- "具有重要意义", "提供深刻启示", "彰显时代价值" without explaining what the meaning, insight, or value is.
- Policy names stacked in one sentence without mechanism.
- "我们青年要..." as a generic ending unrelated to the body.
- "既要...又要...还要..." repeated as formula rather than analysis.
- Grand claims about country, people, history, or youth without evidence.

## AI Smell Audit

Check for:

- Overuse of "此外", "然而", "综上所述", "值得注意的是".
- Three-part lists that do not need three parts.
- Repeated paragraph shape: claim -> explanation -> uplift.
- Empty adjectives: "深刻", "全面", "系统", "有力", "重要".
- Vague authorities: "专家指出", "相关研究表明", "历史证明" without source.
- Formulaic balance: every paragraph says both sides are important but never names the contradiction.

## Style Audit

- Keep literary diction below the density of normal prose. If every sentence has metaphor, remove half.
- Prefer "资本关系的历史界限" over "资本文明星火的时代回响" when discussing theory.
- Use vivid wording to sharpen a point, not to conceal a weak point.
- In academic paragraphs, replace slogan-like phrases with mechanism: who acts, through what institution, under what condition, producing what result.

## Fact And Citation Audit

- Verify recent official documents, law dates, plans, and policy titles.
- Do not fabricate classroom notes, surveys, volunteer experiences, or social-practice records.
- If quoting classics or official speeches, use exact source text or paraphrase without quotation marks.
- Keep references consistent with the user's required citation style.

## Revision Moves

If the paper feels empty:

- Add one named document, event, textbook concept, or case.
- Replace one abstract sentence with a causal explanation.
- Add a small personal observation if the course allows it.

If the paper feels over-decorated:

- Remove metaphors from body paragraphs first.
- Keep the strongest phrase in the opening or conclusion.
- Convert ornate pairs into plain theoretical language.
